⚠   2021 — The Striking Anomaly: Riveredge held $2,509,975 in total liquidity — its highest ever — yet earned only $5,624 in interest (0.22%). The Federal Reserve had cut rates to 0–0.25% in March 2020 in response to COVID-19 and held them there throughout all of 2021. Standard bank accounts paid as little as 0.01%. This was not a bookkeeping error — it was the direct impact of Federal Reserve policy on every institution in America holding cash. By contrast, the same balance in 1990 would have earned approximately $212,000.
Era 1 · High Rate Period
1990–2001
The Fed kept rates elevated to combat inflation. Riveredge earned strong yields of 3–8% on its cash balances. Interest income was a meaningful contributor to the building's income in these years.
Avg yield: 4.44% · Total earned: $229,121
Era 2 · Rate Collapse
2002–2013
The dot-com crash (2001), the 2008 financial crisis, and subsequent quantitative easing drove rates to historic lows. Yields collapsed from 4% to below 1% even as the building's cash balance grew substantially.
Avg yield: 1.09% · Total earned: $94,068
Era 3 · Near-Zero & COVID
2014–2022
Rates crept up briefly in 2018–2019 then were slashed again for COVID in 2020. The building accumulated its largest ever liquidity position — over $2.5M — while earning virtually nothing on it. 2021 produced only $5,624 on $2.5M.
Avg yield: 0.83% · Total earned: $179,318
Era 4 · Rising Rates
2023–2024
The Fed raised rates aggressively from 2022 to combat post-COVID inflation. Interest income surged — $76,136 in 2023 and $53,776 in 2024. The building is finally being compensated for its substantial cash position.
Avg yield: 3.00% · Total earned: $129,912
