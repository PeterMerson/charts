Why this tool exists · Two documented examples

When the numbers tell
a different story.

On two separate occasions — 2006 and 2023 — Riveredge shareholders received memos announcing maintenance increases justified by cost projections that the audited annual financial statements do not support. Both cases are documented here using only the building's own numbers. Neither is offered to assign blame. Both are offered as the reason independent verification matters.

Board members are volunteers who serve with good intentions. This is offered not to assign blame, but to make one point: the audited reports exist so that any shareholder can verify any claim, at any time. These are two examples of why that matters.
— Peter Merson, Apt 8T · Former board member who voted no on the 2006 increase
Example 1 of 2
April 2006  ·  The Record 17% Increase
📄 Read Original Memo →
What the audited reports show · 2002 – 2006
Act I
2002
A legitimate capital need
A roof and facade required major work. A $350,000 shareholder loan was taken, due October 2006. A $2.25/share/year special assessment was levied to fund the project and retire the loan. A sound and reasonable plan.
Act II
2002 – 2005
A slow, visible failure
Assessment funds were used for operating expenses — the exact thing the board's own memo would later admit the money was "not meant" for. The audited reports showed deteriorating cash balances each year. The bylaw obligation to share annual cash needs with shareholders was not met. No one raised the alarm — including the author of this document.
Act III
April 2006
A memo that didn't match
Six months before the loan came due, shareholders received a memo blaming steep energy, insurance, and tax increases for the shortfall. The audited financials show utilities fell in 2006, insurance rose less than 1.4%, and payroll declined. The four-year diversion of assessment funds went unmentioned.
The one true statement in the memo — and the most revealing
"The current assessment was put in place to pay for the facade repair and new roof. It was not meant to be used for ongoing operating expenses."
— Riveredge Board of Directors · April 8, 2006
This is the only factually accurate statement in the memo — and it is also a quiet confession. The board correctly describes what the assessment was supposed to do, without mentioning that the board itself used the money for operating expenses instead. They described the problem, attributed it to external cost pressures, and imposed a record increase — without disclosing the actual cause.

17 / 18 Post-2006 surplus years
The building ran an operating surplus in 17 of the 18 years from 2007 to 2024, totaling over $5.5 million in cumulative net income. This is the opposite of a building under financial distress.

Had the board retired the $350,000 loan with a short-term surcharge and returned maintenance to its prior rate, the building would still have run consistent surpluses — and shareholders would not have paid $1,442/year extra (per 544-share apartment) for 19 years.
⚖   Riveredge's co-op by-laws require the Board of Directors to provide shareholders with the co-op's annual cash needs. This requirement was not met during 2002–2006, nor has it been consistently met since. Had it been honored, the deteriorating cash position would have been visible and addressable years before it became a crisis.
A note on shared responsibility
The board culture at the time discouraged questioning the treasurer's financial decisions. That was a mistake — and one I participated in. The audited reports showing the building's deteriorating cash position were available every year from 2002 onward. Any board member, including myself, could have reviewed them and raised questions earlier.

Good governance is not about distrust. It is about verification. The purpose of this entire suite of tools is to make that verification effortless for every Riveredge shareholder, permanently.
Questions shareholders are entitled to ask
  • 01If energy costs drove the 2006 shortfall, why do the audited reports show utilities fell that year?
  • 02Where did the special assessment funds collected 2002–2006 go, and why wasn't the $350,000 loan reserved?
  • 03Why was a permanent $2.65/share/year increase imposed rather than a temporary surcharge to retire the $350,000 loan?
  • 04Why was the shortfall only identified in April 2006 when the audited reports show deficits dating to 2002?
  • 05Has the board met its bylaw obligation to provide shareholders with the co-op's annual cash needs — and if not, when will it?
  • 06Can the board provide a reconciliation of the memo's stated cost drivers against the audited line items for 2005 and 2006?
Example 2 of 2
November 2023  ·  The 9% Increase
📄 Read Original Memo → 📊 Actual vs. Projection →

History repeated itself in November 2023. The board issued a letter projecting dramatic cost increases to justify a 9% maintenance increase effective January 1, 2024. The 2024 audited annual results — once a one-time fire and water damage event is properly excluded — tell a different story.

Board letter · November 27, 2023
"The Board projects dramatic increases in the cost of operating the Riveredge building and property in 2024."
— Westchester Property Management Group Inc. on behalf of Riveredge Board · Nov. 27, 2023
The letter cited four specific projected increases totalling $218,800: fuel oil +$110,000 · insurance +$23,000 · real estate taxes +$8,500 · other expenses +$77,300. A 9% maintenance increase — $1.58/share/year — was imposed on this basis, generating approximately $134,342 in additional annual income.

In both 2006 and 2023, the cost projections used to justify maintenance increases were not supported by the audited annual results. In 2006, cited cost drivers were flat or falling. In 2023, the board overstated projected increases by 110% — with the single largest item, fuel oil, coming in at one-third of what was projected.

This is not offered as proof of intent. It is offered as proof that independent verification of board financial claims — using the building's own audited statements — is both possible and necessary. The matrix makes that verification available to every shareholder, permanently.
